What are the different types of business loans?

Quick take: Unlike personal loans, business loan approval hinges heavily on your business's financial documentation - clean, well-maintained books can be just as important as revenue itself.

"Business loan" is an umbrella term covering several distinct products, each suited to a different need. A working capital loan covers day-to-day operational expenses - inventory, salaries, short-term cash flow gaps - and is typically a revolving credit line rather than a lump sum. A term loan, on the other hand, is a fixed amount disbursed upfront for a specific purpose like buying equipment or expanding premises, repaid over a set tenure through EMIs.

Many lenders and government-backed schemes also offer collateral-free business loans up to a certain amount, aimed particularly at small and medium enterprises (SMEs) and first-time entrepreneurs who may not have significant assets to pledge. These schemes typically rely more heavily on business financials, cash flow history, and sometimes credit guarantee mechanisms rather than physical collateral.

Lenders evaluate business loan applications very differently from personal or home loans. Instead of just an individual's salary slip, they look at the business's vintage (how long it has been operating), turnover trends, profitability, existing debt obligations, banking transaction history, and often the promoter's personal credit score as well, since many business loans require personal guarantees from the owners.

Interest rates and loan amounts vary widely based on the business's risk profile - a well-established company with strong financials and collateral will get significantly better terms than a new business with limited track record. This is why many first-time entrepreneurs start with smaller, unsecured loans or government scheme-backed loans before qualifying for larger, cheaper secured business credit as their business matures.

Who is eligible for a business loan?

Criteria Typical Requirement
Business vintage Typically 1-3+ years of operational history, varies by lender and scheme
Turnover Minimum annual turnover thresholds set by each lender's policy
Business type Proprietorship, partnership, LLP, or private limited company, all generally eligible
Credit score Both business credit history and promoter's personal score are reviewed
Profitability Consistent or improving profit trend strengthens the application
Existing debt High existing business debt relative to turnover may reduce eligible amount

How a business loan application is assessed

  1. Determine whether you need working capital, a term loan, or equipment financing based on your actual need.
  2. Prepare financial statements - profit and loss, balance sheet, GST returns, and bank statements.
  3. Apply with the lender, providing business registration proof and promoter KYC documents.
  4. The lender evaluates business vintage, turnover, cash flow stability, and credit history of both the business and its promoters.
  5. For secured loans, collateral valuation is conducted; for collateral-free schemes, the assessment relies more on financials.
  6. Upon approval, a sanction letter specifies loan amount, tenure, interest rate, and any guarantee requirements.
  7. Funds are disbursed as a lump sum (term loan) or as a credit line you can draw against (working capital).

Documents typically required

  • Business registration certificate / GST registration
  • PAN card of business and promoters
  • Bank statements (12 months)
  • Income tax returns (business and promoters)
  • Profit and loss statement, balance sheet
  • KYC documents of all partners/directors
  • Collateral documents (if applicable)
Watch out: A personal guarantee, common in small business loans, means your personal assets could be at risk if the business defaults - understand this clause fully before signing, even on an otherwise collateral-free loan.

However, many lenders and government schemes offer collateral-free business loans up to a defined limit, particularly for smaller ticket sizes, relying instead on business financials and personal guarantees.

Lenders assess a combination of factors: business vintage, turnover and profitability trends, existing debt levels, banking relationship history, collateral (if any), and the promoter's personal credit score. Businesses with stronger, more consistent financials and collateral typically secure lower rates than newer or riskier ventures.

For secured loans, the lender can recover dues by liquidating the pledged collateral. For loans backed by a personal guarantee, the lender can pursue the promoter's personal assets as well. Persistent default also severely damages both the business's and promoter's credit history, affecting future borrowing for years.

Most lenders allow prepayment, though foreclosure charges may apply, particularly for term loans, and can range from 1-4% of the outstanding amount. Working capital lines often have different, usage-based cost structures rather than a traditional prepayment penalty. Always check the specific terms in your sanction letter.

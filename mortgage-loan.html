What is a mortgage loan and how is it different from a home loan?

Quick take: A mortgage loan is not the same as a home loan - it's borrowing against a property you already own, for any purpose, not financing the purchase of a new one.

A mortgage loan - more precisely called a Loan Against Property (LAP) - lets you borrow money by pledging a property you already own, whether residential or commercial, as collateral. Unlike a home loan, which is specifically for purchasing or constructing a property, a mortgage loan can be used for virtually any purpose: business expansion, medical emergencies, children's education, debt consolidation, or any other significant expense.

Because the end-use isn't restricted or verified as strictly as a home loan, and because the property already exists and is easier to value accurately (versus an under-construction property), lenders treat mortgage loans as a moderately higher-risk product than home loans, resulting in interest rates that are typically somewhat higher than home loan rates, though still considerably lower than unsecured personal loans.

The loan amount you can access depends on the Loan-to-Value ratio applied to your property's current market value, commonly ranging from 50-70% for residential properties and often lower for commercial properties, reflecting the lender's more conservative view of end-use-unrestricted lending. The property must have a clear, undisputed title, and the lender will conduct both a legal verification and a technical valuation before approval.

Tenure for mortgage loans is generally shorter than home loans, often capping around 15 years compared to the 30-year tenures sometimes available on home loans, though this varies by lender and borrower profile. Because you continue to own and can typically continue living in or using the mortgaged property throughout the loan tenure - unlike, say, a pledged gold loan - many borrowers find this an attractive way to access large sums of capital without liquidating an asset.

How a mortgage loan gets processed

  1. Determine the loan amount needed and confirm your property's approximate market value.
  2. Apply with the lender, submitting property documents, income proof, and identity/address KYC.
  3. The lender conducts a legal title search to confirm the property is free of disputes or existing encumbrances.
  4. A technical valuation team assesses the property's current market value and condition.
  5. Based on the applicable LTV ratio and your income eligibility, the lender issues a sanction letter.
  6. Loan agreement is signed, mortgage is legally registered, and funds are disbursed to your account.
  7. You continue using or occupying the property as before, while repaying the loan through scheduled EMIs.

Documents typically required

  • PAN card and Aadhaar card
  • Property title deed and ownership documents
  • Encumbrance certificate
  • Approved building plan / occupancy certificate
  • Income proof - salary slips or ITR
  • Bank statements (6 months)
  • Property tax receipts
Watch out: Because your property is the collateral, defaulting on a mortgage loan puts a roof over your head at genuine risk - only borrow an amount whose EMI you can comfortably sustain even through a temporary income disruption.

How does an education loan actually work?

Quick take: The moratorium period is an education loan's biggest advantage - it lets you focus on studies now and start repaying only once you have an income, not while you're still a student.

An education loan is designed specifically to fund tuition fees, hostel or living expenses, books, equipment, and sometimes travel costs for studying in India or abroad. What sets it apart from most other loans is the moratorium period - you are not required to start repaying immediately after disbursement.

The moratorium typically covers the full course duration plus an additional 6-12 months after completion (or after securing a job, whichever is earlier). During this period, interest still accrues on the loan, but many lenders allow you to pay only the interest portion, or nothing at all, until the moratorium ends - at which point EMIs on the full principal begin.

For loan amounts up to a certain threshold, commonly around Rs 4-7.5 lakh depending on the lender, education loans are often available without collateral, sometimes with a co-applicant (usually a parent) providing income backing instead. Beyond that threshold, lenders typically require collateral - which could be a fixed deposit, property, or other security - along with a co-applicant.

Interest rates on education loans vary based on the institution's reputation (loans for premier institutes are often priced lower), the course, the country of study, and whether the loan is secured or unsecured. Government-backed interest subsidy schemes also exist in many countries for economically weaker sections during the moratorium period, which is worth checking before you assume you'll pay full interest from day one.

Who is eligible for an education loan?

Criteria Typical Requirement
Applicant Indian resident student with confirmed admission to a recognised institution
Co-applicant Usually a parent, spouse, or sibling with stable income (required in most cases)
Course type Full-time recognised degree/diploma programs, typically not part-time or short courses
Collateral Not required for smaller amounts; required beyond the lender's specified threshold
Academic record Reasonable consistency in past academic performance is usually reviewed
Age Typically 16-35 years, varies by lender and course level

Steps to secure an education loan

  1. Get your admission confirmation letter from the institution - this is mandatory before applying.
  2. Estimate the total cost of the course including tuition, hostel, and living expenses to decide the loan amount.
  3. Apply with a co-applicant (usually a parent), submitting income proof and academic records.
  4. For higher amounts, arrange collateral documentation such as property papers or FD receipts.
  5. The lender assesses the course, institution reputation, and co-applicant's repayment capacity before sanctioning.
  6. Disbursement usually happens directly to the institution for tuition fees, and periodically for living expenses.
  7. Repayment (EMI) begins after the moratorium period ends, based on the agreed repayment schedule.

Documents typically required

  • Admission letter from the institution
  • Fee structure / cost estimate from the college
  • Academic mark sheets and certificates
  • Co-applicant's income proof and ITR
  • Identity and address proof of student and co-applicant
  • Collateral documents (if applicable)
  • Passport and visa (for studying abroad)
Watch out: Interest keeps accruing during the moratorium even if you're not paying it. If you can afford to pay simple interest during your study years, it significantly reduces the total interest burden once full EMIs begin.

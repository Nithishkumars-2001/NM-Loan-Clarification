How does a car loan work?

Quick take: The on-road price - not the ex-showroom price - is what your loan should be calculated against, since registration, insurance, and accessories add meaningfully to the total cost.

A car loan is a secured loan where the vehicle you're purchasing itself serves as collateral, through a process called hypothecation. The lender's charge on the vehicle is recorded in the registration certificate (RC), and this hypothecation is only removed once you fully repay the loan - until then, you cannot legally sell the vehicle without the lender's consent.

For new cars, lenders typically finance 80-90% of the on-road price, which includes the ex-showroom price, registration, insurance, and other charges - not just the sticker price of the vehicle. The remaining amount is your down payment. For used cars, the financing percentage is usually lower, often 60-80% of the assessed value, because used vehicles depreciate faster and carry more valuation uncertainty.

Interest rates on new car loans are generally lower than used car loans, reflecting the lower risk lenders associate with a new, verifiably-valued asset versus a used one with unknown maintenance history. Tenure for car loans typically ranges from 1 to 7 years, and shorter tenures, while resulting in higher EMIs, significantly reduce the total interest paid over the loan's life.

One factor unique to car loans is depreciation - a car loses value the moment it's driven out of the showroom, and continues depreciating throughout the loan tenure. This means that especially in the early years of a long-tenure loan, your outstanding loan amount can actually exceed the car's current market value, a situation informally called being "upside down" on the loan - worth keeping in mind if you're considering longer tenures to lower your EMI.

Steps in getting a car loan approved

  1. Decide on the vehicle and get an on-road price quote (or valuation, for a used car) from the dealer or seller.
  2. Compare loan offers from banks, NBFCs, and dealer-tied financiers - rates and processing fees vary meaningfully.
  3. Submit income proof, KYC documents, and the vehicle quotation or valuation report.
  4. The lender verifies your income, credit score, and (for used cars) the vehicle's condition and market value.
  5. Upon approval, a sanction letter is issued specifying the loan amount, interest rate, and tenure.
  6. The loan amount is disbursed directly to the dealer or seller, and the vehicle's RC reflects the lender's hypothecation.
  7. EMIs begin the following month, and the hypothecation is removed from the RC only after full repayment.

Documents typically required

  • PAN card and Aadhaar card
  • Salary slips or income tax returns
  • Bank statements (3-6 months)
  • Vehicle quotation (new) or valuation report (used)
  • Address proof
  • Passport-size photograph
  • Driving license (often required, not always mandatory for the loan itself)
Watch out: Dealer-arranged financing is convenient but not always the cheapest - always compare the dealer's offered rate against at least one or two independent bank or NBFC quotes before signing.

Since the vehicle is hypothecated, the lender has the legal right to repossess it after sustained default and due notice, then sell it to recover the outstanding dues. If the sale proceeds don't cover the full amount owed, you may still be liable for the shortfall, in addition to the credit score damage from the default.

Comprehensive vehicle insurance is mandatory for financed vehicles, but it's a separate product from the loan itself, even if the dealer or lender offers to bundle it for convenience. It's worth comparing standalone insurance quotes, since bundled options aren't always the most competitively priced.

Most lenders allow prepayment or foreclosure after an initial lock-in period, though a foreclosure charge, often a percentage of the outstanding principal, may apply. Since car loan interest is front-loaded in many amortization structures, prepaying earlier in the tenure typically saves more interest than prepaying later.

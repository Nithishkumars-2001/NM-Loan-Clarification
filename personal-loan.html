Personal Loan Clarification

Fast money, but only if you understand the real cost.

Personal loans are quick, flexible, and unsecured - which also makes them the most expensive form of common borrowing. Here's what actually determines your rate, and where people get caught out.

What makes a personal loan different?

Quick take: Personal loans typically carry the second-highest interest rates after credit cards, precisely because there's no asset backing them. Only borrow what you can comfortably repay within the tenure.

A personal loan is an unsecured loan - you don't pledge any asset like property or gold against it. The lender's only assurance that you'll repay is your income stability and credit history. Because there's no collateral to fall back on if you default, lenders price in that extra risk through a noticeably higher interest rate compared to secured loans like home or car loans.

The upside is speed and flexibility. There's no property valuation, no vehicle registration, no gold appraisal - just income verification and a credit check. Many lenders can disburse a personal loan within 24-48 hours for pre-approved or digitally verified customers, which is why it's the go-to option for medical emergencies, weddings, travel, debt consolidation, or any expense that can't wait.

Because there's no restriction on end-use (unlike a home or car loan, which is tied to a specific asset), lenders compensate by scrutinising your income-to-obligation ratio closely and by capping loan amounts more conservatively relative to your salary, typically anywhere from 10 to 24 times your monthly income depending on the lender's policy and your credit profile.

Interest rates on personal loans are almost always fixed for the entire tenure, which typically ranges from 12 to 60 months. This predictability is useful for budgeting, but it also means you're locked into whatever rate you're offered at the time of approval - there's no benefit if market rates fall later, unless you refinance.

How a personal loan gets approved

  1. Check your eligibility and compare offers across lenders - even a 1-2% rate difference matters over the loan's life.
  2. Submit KYC documents, income proof, and bank statements, either online or at a branch.
  3. The lender pulls your credit report and assesses your Debt-to-Income ratio and repayment history.
  4. Based on risk profile, the lender issues a sanction with the approved amount, tenure, interest rate, and processing fee.
  5. You e-sign the loan agreement and the amount is disbursed directly to your bank account.
  6. EMIs are auto-debited monthly from the linked account through the agreed tenure.

Documents typically required

  • PAN card and Aadhaar card
  • Latest 3 months' salary slips
  • Bank statements (3-6 months)
  • Employment ID or offer letter
  • Income tax returns (for self-employed)
  • Passport-size photograph
Watch out: Watch for the processing fee (often 1-3% of the loan amount) and any prepayment or foreclosure charges - these can add meaningfully to your total cost even if the headline interest rate looks attractive.
